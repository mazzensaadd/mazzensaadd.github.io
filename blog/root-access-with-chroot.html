Root Access with Chroot

One night, I was tinkering around with my laptop doing an Archlinux install (pretty typical night, isn’t it?) and I was reading through the Archwiki page of the chroot command. If you’ve done an Arch install, you know what’s chroot, but if you didn’t, let me explain.

chroot enables you to change the apparent root directory for the current running process and their children. But what the heck does this mean? I will tell you. Let’s consider a situation where your grub.cfg file has been fucked up for some unknown reason and you can’t boot into the system. You know that the system is still there but you can’t boot into it because the bootloader is missing. What would you do? Wouldn’t it be great if there was a way to gain access to your OS without needing the bootloader at all? Then you can fix it and get everything up and running again, right? This is what chroot does. You boot your laptop using a bootable USB stick, running Linux of course. Then, you mount the partition containing the root directory of your OS. After that, you use the chroot command to change root into the disk you just mounted. Now, you have root privileges on this os without needing to open it. From here, you can fix your laptop and get grub working again.

My thought was: “What if, instead of using it to fix broken laptops, I use it to break into laptops? Wouldn’t this work just fine?”. This was it. I grabbed my USB and flashed Endeavour OS on it. I then shut down my laptop and booted into the USB stick. I mounted the correct partition and chrooted into it and voalá, I now have root privileges on my machine without even needing to enter a single password.

I felt so powerful at this moment. I thought I have breached the Linux Security Myth. Now, I can open anyone’s laptop and gain root privileges and, from there, I can do lots of things, add a user for me for example, to use his laptop without him knowing. Or I can change his password and mess around with him. I can now hack into any Linux machine that I have physical access to, right? Wrong.

The only possible way to stop such an attack is to encrypt your hard disk (or your SSD if you can afford one xD). Encrypting will prevent anyone random person from mounting your hard and reading it, thus blocking any chroot attempt keeping you and your laptop secure.

So, Kids, encrypt your disks and mess with your friends if you want hehe.
